# Can an Autistic Person with DID Have a "Neurotypical" Alter? A Research Review

## TL;DR

- **The expert consensus is a qualified "no" at the neurological level but "yes" at the behavioral level:** because autism is a lifelong, whole-brain neurodevelopmental condition present from before birth, every alter shares the same autistic brain — so no single alter can be genuinely, neurologically neurotypical. However, alters can differ dramatically in how much they *express* or *mask* autistic traits, so one alter may convincingly *present* as neurotypical while another appears markedly autistic.
- **The most authoritative clinical statement** comes from the International Society for the Study of Trauma and Dissociation (ISSTD), whose Neurodiversity and Dissociation Special Interest Group states plainly that it is "not possible for an individual with DID/OSDD to have only one or a few alters with autism, just as it is not possible for an autistic individual to have one or a few alters without autism" — while affirming that "parts can differ in their presentation due to development, stress levels, and ability to use dissociation."
- **Lived-experience accounts converge with the clinical view:** the best-documented case, autism activist Donna Williams (Polly Samuel), described alters that were "each differently autistic but each still autistic." The key distinction throughout the literature is between (a) a truly neurotypical alter (not supported), (b) an alter that passes as neurotypical through masking or different behavioral patterns (well-documented), and (c) the gap between subjective experience and objective clinical reality.

## Key Findings

1. **Autism is understood as a stable, lifelong, whole-brain neurodevelopmental condition.** It is defined by the DSM-5 as persistent deficits present from the early developmental period, is highly heritable, and is not considered curable. Because it reflects brain development from before birth and is shared by the single physical brain, it cannot logically belong to only some alters.

2. **DID and dissociation are strongly over-represented in autistic people.** In the largest survey to date (Reuben, Stanzione & Singleton, 2021, *Autism in Adulthood*, 687 autistic adults), 94% scored above the clinical cut-off on at least one of six mind-based (psychoform) dissociation subscales, and 32% scored above cut-off for body-based (somatoform) dissociation; 44% met PTSD criteria. Autistic people face very high rates of childhood trauma, a precondition for DID.

3. **Alters can genuinely differ in measurable physiological and functional characteristics** — including handedness, visual acuity, allergies, EEG patterns, and cerebral blood flow — according to a body of case studies and small controlled studies. This makes the question of whether autistic traits can vary across alters scientifically live rather than absurd.

4. **The reconciliation offered by experts is "masking," not "different neurology."** A seemingly neurotypical alter is best understood as a high-masking part of an autistic system, not as a part that genuinely lacks autistic neurology. Parts differ in willingness and ability to mask, and specific alters may consistently "switch out" to handle autism-related stress (e.g., a meltdown), creating the false appearance that only some parts are affected.

5. **There is real scientific uncertainty and genuine controversy** — both about the ontological status of alters (trauma model vs. sociocognitive model of DID) and about the under-researched intersection of autism and dissociation. The strongest claim (a fully neurotypical alter) is not supported by any evidence; the more modest claim (an alter that presents as neurotypical) is well-supported.

## Details

### 1. The core neuroscience/psychology tension

Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) is classified in the DSM-5 as a neurodevelopmental disorder defined by "persisting deficits in social communication and social interaction across multiple contexts, alongside restricted, repetitive patterns" with onset in the early developmental period. It is widely described as a lifelong condition with no cure. A large twin study (Taylor, Gillberg, Lichtenstein & Lundström, 2017, *Molecular Autism*; the CATSS cohort of over 2,500 twin pairs) found that "the heritability of autistic traits was 76–77% in childhood and 60–62% in adulthood," and that "the majority (85%) of the stability in autistic traits was attributable to genetic factors" — underscoring their biological rootedness and lifelong stability. Neuroimaging consortia (e.g., ENIGMA analyses of thousands of individuals) associate autism with widespread, distributed differences in brain structure and cortical asymmetry — i.e., it is a property of the whole brain, not a localized or state-switchable feature.

DID, by contrast, is a dissociative condition in which a single person develops two or more distinct personality states ("alters") as a response to severe, repeated childhood trauma, typically before ages 5–9. The central tension the user identifies is real: if autism is a fixed, whole-brain trait and all alters share one brain, then autism must belong to the whole system, not to individual alters.

Experts resolve this by distinguishing the *underlying neurology* (shared, autistic, unchangeable) from the *behavioral presentation* (variable across alters). All alters "share one brain," so a brain-based condition "affects the whole system, not just one part," but "one alter may act in a way that feels 'autistic'... Another may act more socially. It does not mean only one alter has autism. It means the alters behave differently based on what they carry."

### 2. Clinical and research literature on co-occurrence

The intersection is real but under-researched. Autistic people show markedly elevated rates of trauma, PTSD, and dissociation — the substrate from which DID develops:

- Studies find 32%–60% of autistic adults screen positive for PTSD, though only about 16%–20% report a professional PTSD diagnosis.
- Confirmed abuse rates are very high: one review found 50.4%–80% of autistic people report some form of childhood maltreatment.
- In Reuben et al. (2021, 687 autistic adults), 94% scored above the clinical cut-off on at least one of the six Multiscale Dissociation Inventory subscales — emotional disengagement (85%), depersonalization (72%), emotional constriction (60%), derealization (59%), memory dissociation (53%), and **identity dissociation (26%)** — with 32% above cut-off on the somatoform SDQ-20 and 44% meeting PTSD criteria.

Documented case material specifically pairing ASD (Level 1) with DID exists but is sparse. A 2025 case report in *Psychopharmacology Bulletin* (Nandipati & Reddy) describes a 17-year-old with ASD Level 1, gender dysphoria, and DID, and claims to be the first case report of these three as comorbid diagnoses — indicating how thin the formal case literature is. Several clinical/commercial mental-health sites discuss the co-occurrence as real but "relatively unexplored" and "rare."

**Directionality:** The ISSTD notes the differential is genuinely difficult — autism can overshadow PTSD/DID and vice versa, because both involve overlapping features (emotional dysregulation, differences in eye contact, sensory issues vs. hypervigilance, alexithymia vs. depersonalization). It also flags the *reverse* risk: autistic people may over-identify with DID (e.g., mistaking maladaptive daydreaming or richly developed internal worlds for alters), and people who self-identify with DID may over-identify as autistic. West, Somer & Eigsti (2022, *Imagination, Cognition and Personality*) found that "experiences of immersive daydreaming were identified in 42% of adults with ASD," with 43% of autistic participants meeting proposed maladaptive-daydreaming criteria — a phenomenon that can be mistaken for plurality.

### 3. State-dependent variation across alters

There is a documented (if methodologically contested) research literature showing alters can differ in measurable physiological and functional characteristics:

- **Visual/optical function:** Miller (1989, *Journal of Nervous and Mental Disease*, 177(8):480–486) examined 9 MPD patients and 9 controls role-playing MPD across "eight optical measures in four prominent areas of vision," and reported that "MPD subjects had significantly more variability in visual functioning across alter personalities than did control subjects." A replication (Miller, Blackburn, Scholes, White & Mamalis, 1991, same journal, 179(3):132–135) with 20 patients and 20 role-playing controls broadly confirmed more differences in MPD subjects, though the abstract explicitly cautioned that findings were "not entirely consistent across the various visual measures or with findings from previous studies." Birnbaum & Thomann (1996, *Journal of the American Optometric Association*, 67(6):327–334) reviewed reported differences across alters in "visual acuity, refraction, oculomotor status, visual field, color vision, corneal curvature, pupil size, and intraocular pressure," including "personality-specific strabismus" and, in their own case, "personality-specific hemianopsia and refractive differences... of sufficient magnitude to require the use of different glasses."
- **Broader physiology:** Reviews cite reported differences across alters in handedness, medication response, allergic sensitivities, autonomic and endocrine function, EEG, visual evoked potentials, and regional cerebral blood flow.
- **Neuroimaging:** Reinders et al. ("One brain, two selves," 2003, *NeuroImage*; and later PET/fMRI studies through 2014 and 2022) demonstrated distinct patterns of regional cerebral blood flow and "opposite brain emotion-regulation patterns" for different self-states, and a 2012 study attempted to distinguish authentic from simulated DID states.

**On autism specifically:** No controlled study has tested whether autistic *traits* vary across alters. The evidence is clinical and anecdotal. The ISSTD notes that Donna Williams's alters had "different language abilities, sensory processing, ability to navigate social situations, tics, and learning delays" — and frames this as parts differing in *presentation and masking*, not in underlying neurology. An alter can plausibly mask autistic traits more effectively, appear more socially fluent, or have more developed coping/compensation skills, without any alter genuinely lacking the autism.

**Crucial caveat:** The literal interpretation of physiological differences is heavily disputed. Merckelbach et al. (2002, *Clinical Psychology Review*) argue these studies suffer from "an over-reliance on the single-subject, case-study design, as well as a lack of adequate experimental controls," and that role-playing controls can reproduce many effects. So even where differences are real, they may reflect state-dependent psychophysiology, attention, arousal, or expectation — not literally different bodies or brains. This is directly relevant: even a measurable difference in how "autistic" an alter appears would not prove one alter is neurologically neurotypical.

### 4. The masking/camouflaging distinction

Masking (or camouflaging) is the conscious or unconscious suppression of autistic traits to appear neurotypical — forcing eye contact, rehearsing scripts, hiding sensory discomfort, mimicking others. It is measured by tools like the Camouflaging Autistic Traits Questionnaire (CAT-Q) and modeled (Hull et al.) as three components: compensation, masking, and assimilation. It is more common in women and gender-diverse autistic people and is linked to exhaustion, anxiety, burnout, and "identity confusion."

This maps directly onto the "neurotypical alter" question. The ISSTD explicitly connects masking to identity fragmentation: "Pressure to 'mask' as neurotypical... may cause identity confusion and, when combined with childhood trauma, encourage the formation of dissociated identities." It describes how, for some autistic people, "trauma may provide strong pressure to 'mask as neurotypical'," and notes DID systems can contain "self-blaming high-masking parts conflicting with parts who are severely affected." It also observes that "certain parts may consistently switch out in response to unique autism-related stressors (e.g., an alter always switching out to have a meltdown in response to sensory overload) so that it falsely appears that only those parts are affected."

The interpretive upshot: a seemingly neurotypical alter is best understood as **an autistic system engaging in sophisticated, part-specific masking**, not as a part that genuinely lacks autistic neurology. Multiple autistic self-advocates describe autistic masking and dissociation as deeply intertwined, with the "shattering" of a lifelong mask sometimes resembling or overlapping with switching.

### 5. Perspectives from the autistic and plural communities

Lived-experience accounts are notably consistent with the clinical framing — and often more nuanced:

- **Donna Williams / Polly Samuel** (Australian autism author, 1963–2017; diagnosed autistic in 1991 and with DID in 2010) is the most-cited case. She wrote: "My autism has shaped the nature of my alters, each one is differently autistic but each is still autistic." Her alters differed in language (some echolalic, some selectively mute, one largely aphasic), abilities, and roles — but she and her interlocutor "Jennifer" both insisted all parts were autistic, with Jennifer saying: "YES all my parts have Autism, they just all handle it differently," and comparing the question "Are all your parts Autistic?" to asking "Are all your parts Caucasian?"
- **Plural bloggers** describe overlap plus variation: "there is a lot of overlap with our autistic traits, but some alters have autistic traits not shared with everyone... Everyone masks in their own ways... Some of us can maintain eye contact longer." They also note the whole body's abilities/disabilities are shared, "some are just better at managing/putting up with them than others."
- A UK Autism Education Trust contributor ("Autistic Selves," diagnosed autistic at 42) described developing alters partly *as a way of coping with being autistic* in a chaotic world, with each alter holding a distinct role.

The plural community also distinguishes DID/OSDD from "endogenic" or non-traumagenic plurality and pushes back on purely pathologizing framings — relevant context, though the clinical question here concerns trauma-based DID.

### 6. Expert and clinician views

- **ISSTD (trauma/dissociation experts):** the clearest institutional statement — parts can't split cleanly into "autistic" and "non-autistic"; autism is a consistent underlying condition; differences reflect development, stress, and masking.
- **Dissociation neuroscience (Reinders and colleagues):** alters correspond to real, measurable differences in brain *function/state* — but this speaks to distinct self-states, not to distinct fixed neurotypes.
- **Sociocognitive-model skeptics (Lilienfeld, Lynn, Spanos, Merckelbach):** argue alters are not literally separate indwelling people but "enactments" shaped by suggestion, expectation, media, and sometimes therapy. Lilienfeld characterized DID as "a disorder of belief," and this camp holds that people with DID "are not, in reality, a conglomeration of indwelling entities, despite their subjective conviction." On this view, the very premise of a "neurotypical alter" as a separate biological entity is misconceived — there is one autistic person with varying self-state enactments.
- Notably, both major camps (trauma model and sociocognitive model) agree DID is a genuine disorder of self-perception and that alters are not separate biological organisms — which independently rules out one alter being a different neurotype.

### 7. Terminology note

"Asperger's Syndrome" was introduced in DSM-IV (1994) and eliminated in DSM-5 (2013), folded into the single category Autism Spectrum Disorder; most people formerly diagnosed with Asperger's map onto "ASD Level 1." ICD-11 (effective 2022) similarly removed it. The DSM-5 severity "levels" are widely opposed by the autistic community and were not adopted by ICD-11. Some people retain "Asperger's" or "Aspie" as personal identity terms.

There are two framings in tension throughout this topic: the **medical/pathologizing** frame (autism as a disorder with deficits) and the **neurodiversity** frame (autism as a natural, lifelong variation in the human brain, not something to be cured). Both, notably, agree autism is intrinsic and lifelong — so neither supports a genuinely non-autistic alter. The neurodiversity frame in fact strengthens the "no" answer, since it treats autism as identity-level neurology rather than a removable symptom set.

### 8. Nuances and competing views — the three-way distinction

The literature supports carefully separating:

- **(a) A genuinely neurotypical alter (different neurology):** Not supported by any evidence and rejected by clinical consensus. All alters share one autistic brain.
- **(b) An alter that presents/passes as neurotypical (masking / different behavior):** Well-documented and plausible. Alters differ in masking ability, social fluency, coping skills, and which stressors they absorb. This is the correct reading of nearly all "neurotypical alter" reports.
- **(c) Subjective experience vs. objective clinical reality:** A system may sincerely experience one alter as "the neurotypical one." That subjective reality is meaningful and should be respected clinically, but it does not establish that the alter is neurologically neurotypical. Both the trauma and sociocognitive models agree the subjective conviction of separate selves does not equal literally separate biological persons.

## Recommendations

**For a person (or system) with both diagnoses wondering about a "neurotypical alter":**

1. **Reframe the question from neurology to presentation.** The productive question is not "is this alter neurotypical?" (almost certainly no) but "why does this alter mask so well, and at what cost?" High-masking parts are common and can carry heavy anxiety and burnout.
2. **Watch for the masking–burnout cost.** If a "neurotypical" alter is doing the heavy lifting of social performance, expect fatigue, meltdowns absorbed by other parts, and identity strain. This is a treatment target, not a sign that autism is absent.
3. **Ensure both conditions are assessed and treated — don't let one overshadow the other.** The ISSTD warns that attributing everything to trauma leaves neurodevelopmental needs unmet (and vice versa). Seek clinicians competent in *both* dissociation and autism, or willing to consult across silos (tools like the Coventry Grid can help the differential).

**For clinicians:**
4. **Treat the whole person as autistic across all states,** while individualizing support to each part's masking capacity and stressors. Adapt dissociation-focused techniques (grounding, visualization, EMDR) to the person's sensory and cognitive profile, since concrete thinkers or aphantasics may not respond to standard imagery-based methods.
5. **Distinguish genuine parts-based dissociation from autistic phenomena that mimic it** — maladaptive daydreaming, rich internal worlds, alexithymia, depersonalization — before concluding DID, especially given social-media-driven self-identification.

**Benchmarks that would change these recommendations:** A controlled study directly measuring validated autism metrics (e.g., ADOS-2, sensory and social-cognition tasks) *across* alters within the same DID individuals — showing one state reliably scoring in the non-autistic range on objective, hard-to-fake measures — would be needed to revise the current "no genuinely neurotypical alter" conclusion. No such study currently exists.

## Caveats

- **This is an under-researched intersection.** Almost all direct evidence on autism-across-alters is clinical observation or lived experience (notably a single prominent case, Donna Williams), not controlled research. Treat firm claims in either direction with caution.
- **Lived-experience accounts are valuable but are not clinical evidence** and are clearly distinguished as such here. They are, however, strikingly consistent with the expert view that all parts share the autism.
- **DID itself is scientifically contested.** The trauma model and the sociocognitive model disagree sharply about what alters *are*. This review does not adjudicate that debate; it notes that both models converge on rejecting the idea of one alter being a literally distinct neurotype.
- **Physiological-difference studies across alters are real but methodologically weak** (small samples, single-case designs, weak controls, possible demand characteristics). They should not be over-read as proof that alters have different brains or bodies.
- **Terminology and framing are value-laden.** "Neurotypical," "disorder," "alter," and "system" carry different meanings in clinical vs. community contexts; this report uses them descriptively, not to endorse a particular ideology.
